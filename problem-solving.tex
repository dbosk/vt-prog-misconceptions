\subsection{Problem solving}

\subsubsection{Role in the syllabus}

Translating a problem --- often a mathematical one --- into working code is a
central activity of CS1, and one where novices frequently struggle independently
of their grasp of the individual language constructs.

\subsubsection{Difficulties that can occur}

Mathematical problems are particularly demanding for novices.
\textcite{KumarVeerasamy2016} found that students did markedly worse on
math-related tasks:
\blockcquote{KumarVeerasamy2016}{This study analysis also explored that novices
of programming struggled in writing code for math-related Questions 6 and 7.
Nearly 66\% of students did not do well in the mathematical problem-based
questions though explained and allowed to surf the Internet to seek for more
details during the exam hours. A neo-Piagetian theory of cognitive development
stated that students who are at the concrete operational stage struggle to write
large programs with partial specifications, although they can write small
programs from well-defined specifications.}

A related difficulty is debugging: students struggle to trace code and locate
errors, and in particular to reason about the order in which a program will
execute \parencite[p.~20]{Sleeman1984}. The same order-of-execution difficulty
is reported by \textcite{Kurvinen2016}. How lab instructions should be framed so
that students take the intended knowledge from an exercise is a further question
we leave open here.
